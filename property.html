Property Laws

Property tax is a levy imposed by the local government on property owners within its jurisdiction. It is a major source of revenue for municipal bodies and is used to fund various civic amenities and services, such as road maintenance, garbage collection, and street lighting. Here's a detailed description of property tax in India

1. Ownership:

Indian law recognizes two types of property ownership - ancestral property and self-acquired property.
Ancestral property refers to property inherited up to four generations of male lineage and should be equally divided among legal heirs. Self-acquired property is one that a person acquires through their own efforts or resources, and they have the right to dispose of it as they wish.

2. Succession:

In case of intestate succession (when a person dies without a will), the property is divided among legal heirs according to the personal laws applicable to them (Hindu Succession Act for Hindus, Indian Succession Act for Christians, etc.).
- Making a will can help in specifying how one's property should be distributed after death.

3. Title Verification:

Before buying a property, it's crucial to verify the title deed and ensure that the seller has the legal right to sell the property. This can help avoid legal disputes later.

4. Stamp duty and registration:

Property transactions in India require payment of stamp duty and registration fees. These fees vary from state to state and are based on the value of the property.

5. Encumbrance Certificate:

It's advisable to obtain an encumbrance certificate from the sub-registrar's office to ensure that the property is free from any legal dues or mortgages.

6. Property Tax

Property owners are required to pay property tax to the local municipal corporation or council. Non-payment of property tax can lead to legal issues and penalties.

7.Tenant Rights:

Tenants in India are protected under the Rent Control Acts of various states. These laws regulate the relationship between landlords and tenants, including rent, eviction, and maintenance of rented premises.

8. Dispute Resolution:

In case of property disputes, individuals can seek legal recourse through civil courts, consumer forums, or alternative dispute resolution mechanisms like mediation and arbitration.
